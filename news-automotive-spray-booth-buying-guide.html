Automotive Spray Booth Buying Guide

How to choose an automotive spray booth for an auto body shop

A spray booth is not only a product purchase. For an auto body shop, it affects refinishing quality, workflow speed, energy use, safety planning, and customer confidence.

Start with the repair shop workflow

Before comparing automotive spray booth prices, a repair shop should define the daily job type. A small collision repair center may need a compact car paint booth for passenger cars. A busy auto body shop that works on SUVs, vans, or commercial vehicles may need more inner height, wider working space, or a longer booth body.

Good purchasing information includes workshop length, width, height, door clearance, target vehicle size, expected daily jobs, power supply, and local exhaust requirements. These details help a spray booth manufacturer recommend a configuration instead of guessing from a basic model name.

Compare airflow, filtration, and lighting

Airflow design is one of the most important parts of an auto body shop spray booth. Down-draft, semi-down-draft, and side-draft designs each fit different building conditions and budget levels. The goal is to move overspray away from the painter and toward the filter system while maintaining a clean refinishing area.

Repair shops should also review ceiling filtration, exhaust filtration, floor filter layout, and lighting placement. Bright LED lighting and stable airflow help technicians inspect surface preparation, basecoat coverage, and clearcoat finish more easily.

Confirm heating and voltage early

Heating choice depends on local energy cost, workshop habit, and country regulations. Electric heating, diesel heating, gas heating, and infrared lamp assistance may all be discussed depending on the project. Voltage, phase, and frequency should be confirmed before production, especially for buyers in Europe, North America, and South America.

A reliable supplier should ask for power information at the quotation stage. This avoids delays later when the booth control system, fan motors, lighting, and heating system need to match the site.

Look for project proof and support

Photos of installed automotive spray booth projects help buyers understand a supplier's export experience. Project images show booth structure, door design, lighting layout, installation condition, and the finish of completed equipment.

For overseas buyers, communication support is just as important as the booth itself. A repair shop should ask about packing, shipping documents, installation drawings, technical guidance, and spare parts. These points make the project easier to manage after the order is placed.
